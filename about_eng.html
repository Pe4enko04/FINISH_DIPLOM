Biography

Screenwriter, Writer, Prose writer

Chingiz Torekulovich Aitmatov - Kyrgyz and Russian writer; diplomat. Hero of Socialist Labor, Hero of the Kirghiz Republic, People's Writer of the Kirghiz SSR, Academician of the Academy of Sciences of the Kirghiz SSR, laureate of the Lenin and three State Prizes of the USSR.

Since his student days, Chingiz Aitmatov has been engaged in translations and translation theory. Aitmatov read a lot, but among the writers he singled out: Ernest Hemingway, Gabriel Garcia Marquez, James Joyce, Marcel Proust, Fazil Iskander. In 2008, he was present at the filming of a documentary called "And the day lasts longer than a century."

Birth

Born on December 12, 1928 in the village of Sheker, Talas Canton of the Kirghiz Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic (now the Talas Region of Kyrgyzstan). His father, Torekul Aitmatov (1903-1938), was at first a peasant activist, then a Soviet and party worker, a prominent statesman of the Kirghiz SSR; in 1937 he was arrested, in 1938 he was shot. Mother, Nagima Abduvalieva (1904-1971),Tatar by nationality, was an army political worker, later a public figure. Genghis, his brothers and sisters grew up in Sheker, where they arrived shortly before their father's arrest at his insistence. During the Great Patriotic War, a fourteen-year-old teenager became the secretary of the council in the village; worked as a teacher, financial agent, assistant combine operator and tractor driver. After graduating from eight classes, he entered the Dzhambul Zootechnical School (now the Kulan Agrotechnical College), which he graduated with honors. In 1948 he entered the Kyrgyz Agricultural Institute in Frunze, from which he graduated in 1953.

Literary activity

He made his debut in print in 1952 with the story “Newsman Juyo” written in Russian (newspaper Komsomolets Kirgizii, April 6, 1952), after which he published stories in Kyrgyz and Russian. After graduating from the institute, he worked as a veterinarian for three years, while continuing to write and publish stories. In 1956 he entered the Higher Literary Courses in Moscow, from which he graduated in 1958. The story "Face to Face" in the Kyrgyz language was published in June 1957 in the magazine "Ala-Too" and the following year in the magazine "October" in the author's translation into Russian. In 1958, the story "Jamilya" was published, which brought Aitmatov world fame (and at the same time translated into French by Louis Aragon). In 1959-1965 he was the editor-in-chief of the Literary Kyrgyzstan magazine, at the same time he worked as his own correspondent for the Pravda newspaper in the Kirghiz SSR. Member of the CPSU since 1959. After "Jamili" the novels "Camel's Eye" (1960), "The First Teacher" (1961), "Mother's Field" (1963) and the collection "The Tale of Mountains and Steppes" (1963) were also published, for which the writer received the Lenin Prize. All these works were published simultaneously in Kyrgyz and Russian translations. In 1965, the story "The First Teacher" was filmed by Andrei Konchalovsky at Mosfilm, and "Camel's Eye" was also filmed by Larisa Shepitko with Bolot Shamshiev in the role of Kemel. The story "Farewell, Gulsary!" (1966) brought Aitmatov the State Prize. Before her, he wrote in two languages ​​(mainly in Kyrgyz), and after that he switched mainly to Russian. The story "The White Steamboat" (1970) was published in Russian; its adaptation was shown at international film festivals in Berlin and Venice. The joint work of Aitmatov with the Kazakh playwright Kaltay Mukhamedzhanov - the play "Climbing Mount Fuji" (1973) - still does not leave the theater stages of Kazakhstan. For the story "Early Cranes" (1975), Aitmatov received the Kyrgyz Toktogul Prize. On August 31, 1973, he signed a letter from a group of Soviet writers about Solzhenitsyn and Sakharov. In 1977, the story "Piebald Dog Running at the Edge of the Sea" was published, later also filmed by German and Russian[specify] filmmakers. In 1978 he was awarded the title of Hero of Socialist Labor. In 1980, the novel “And the Day Lasts Longer than a Century” was published, for which Aitmatov received the second State Prize. The last work published in the USSR is the novel The Scaffold (1986). During a visit to Germany, he met Friedrich Hitzer, later a German translator and manager, with whom he worked until January 2007, when the translator died suddenly of a heart attack.
